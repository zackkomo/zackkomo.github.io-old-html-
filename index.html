EE330: Integrated Electronics - Final Project

During my junior year, through taking EE330, I learned a lot about the manufacturing process for integrated circuits. Along with that, I gained insight in designing different circuits, mainly focusing on amplifiers. For the final project in the course, another student and I completed one of the final projects. Our prompt was to create a device like the Maxim DS 1666, where there are 4 modes of operation. Through a 2-bit input the user will be able to switch between the different modes. In mode 00 it operates like a noninverting finite gain amplifier, in mode 01 like a programmable inverting finite gain amplifier, in mode 10 like a 4-bit DAC and finally in mode 11 like as a potentiometer and operational amplifier. We quickly simplified the process by identifying the common aspects and made a Transmission Gate (bi-directional voltage controlled switch), a 4-to-1 Multiplexer, an Operational Amplifier, a Resistor Array and a 4-to-16 Decoder. Following are the presentation slides and final report that explain the project in more detail. - Project Report

CprE 281: Digital Logic - Final Project

For my Cpre281 Final Project my sophomore year, we were given a choice of a few projects. I decided to go with "Booths Multiplication". The basic idea of the algorithm is a way of multiplying two numbers in two's compliment. A summary of the algorithm is: you start with one of the numbers as a running number and add the multiplier or the multiplicand based on the last two digits of the running number. Each iteration of the process will shift the number to the left and repeat the process for as many times as there are binary digits. For my project specifically, I used the Altera DE2-155 board in such a way that a user would input two 8-bit numbers using 16 switches on the Altera board, and then the multiplication of the two would show up on the HEX display of the board. This project helped me get very familiar with using Digital Logic to get the results I want, as well as designing basic level FSMs (Finite State Machines). For the project I used various collections of systems that I had made throughout the semester, like a Fast Adder, the 7-Segment display output, as well as a Register File to hold the numbers.

Senior Design

In the final year at Iowa State University all ECpE students are part of a senior design team that is tasked with completing a project. My team consisted of a total of 6 people. The name of the project was "Wake-up Harvester Design for Batteryless IoT System" and the website the university provided for it is here.

As the name suggests, our project is to create an Internet of Things device that can be woken up. The project itself is seemingly simple, but the more you look at the constraints it presents the more it starts becoming a project. The purpose of the project is to create a system to support a microprocessing unit. This unit is fully accessible to the user to use as they see fit. To power the MCU, a harvesting unit is required. This allows the whole system to act as a wireless self-reliant system. In order to make this an IoT device, it also has the capability of receiving and transmitting data. Given this setup, the only additional feature is the ability to “wake-up” the device when necessary for synchronization or any reason a user may want to.

My role in the project is fluid, as is everyone in the project. I have worked with everyone on the team to create a high level design of our system. In addition to this, I was tasked with finding a number of components we will end up using in our design. The process helped me gain experience in looking for components that need to match specifications while also being reasonably priced and able to interact with other components in the system. In addition to this, we will be conducting data gathering tests of which I will be in charge of one. After that I will work with Altium PCB Designer to create schematics for parts of the project as well as contribute to the PCB layout of our IoT device.

Overall, my contribution is more focused on helping with confirming the overall design and performing the test to confirm our design’s feasibility. While I have contributed to the overall design, I will probably not play a major role when it comes to the PCB layout. I will need to familiarize myself with the layout, however, since testing will involve me having to know the placement and connections withing the PCB. This will definately increase my knowledge on using Altium PCB Designer and also testing procedures and equipement like Osciloscopes and devices to test the transmission and receiving capabilities. For more details on the process or the project itself, you can refer to our project’s website.

General Education Reflection

One of my biggest goals going through the education system is to get the most I can out of it. Fortunately, I live in a time where the standard is to provide a "well rounded" education where my degree requires me to take courses outside my major, and even outside my discipline. As an engineering student, I felt that my education doesn't talk about a lot of societal situations and more people-oriented education. This is precisely why my 5 general education courses involved 2 courses in sociology (SOC 135 & 331), 1 in psychology (PSYCH 101), 1 in African American studies (AFAM 201)as well as one on Russia in the last 100 years (RUS 375).

In my sociology classes, I learned a lot about how society in general functions, and the underlying reasons that lead to how we function today. Going through a course that talked about the different reforms that happened to transition people from a societal-less existence to slowly forming communities and then further into whole countries helped me put things into perspective. In addition to this, talking about the different types and forms of government, the benefits and detriments of each and how the people benefited from each was important. This gave me the perspective that not everything is perfect, as well as the fact that society keeps changing. Our needs as citizens change and thus the response and support communities provide should also change to reflect that.

In my psychology course I went through the basics of behavioral psychology and how some things can be attributed to biology while other things cannot. One of my favorite activities in the class was having to find an article to read relating to current research in psychology and reading up and writing a short report on it. Being introduced to a field which I have no understanding over, and cannot see myself having an understanding, even though it affects my day to day life provides a new angle of seeing things. Being able to identify my own patterns and breaking from intuition to reevaluate some decisions is an essential skill to have, since my goal is to conduct research on new and cutting edge technology. Being able to take a step back and identify assumptions and biases I have will prove an essential tool in making breakthroughs, even in the simplest of actions.

In the AFAM class I took, I learned much about the history of how Africans were shipped to North America (among other places) as well as the slavery and inhumanities that took place. Being able to understand the past aided in giving me an understanding on current events. During this class, and recent unrest relating to the content covered in the class, I learned a lot about systemic racism as well as other forms. The issue is definitely not a knew one, and clearly is not one that can be solved in an instant. Being exposed to the facts about the past won’t help me understand what some people go through on a daily basis, but can at least put me in a better situation to check myself and carry myself in a way that will not offend or antagonize people around me.

Finally, in the RUS 375 class I learned about how much I don’t know about the world. The reason behind me taking the class is, I had seen it on a list and then realized that I knew absolutely nothing about how Russia came to be, as well as that I knew next to nothing about the Soviet Union. The feeling also translates to my own major, since there are many subfields withing Electrical Engineering that I have not even been exposed to. Going through the class content slowly gave me an understanding on current situations as well as clarifying misconceptions I had about events in the past. In terms of my major, it motivated me to try to familiarize myself with as much as I can about all the different subfields, and be aware that I may have misconceptions about them that may not be true.

All these classes contributed to a more well rounded and reflective education. While taking 5 classes hardly makes me knowledgeable in any of the contents of the classes or their subsequent fields, it helps expose me to new areas that I didn’t have the chance to through my major.

Cumulative Reflection

A large portion of the early 100-level courses I took as an Electrical Engineering student at ISU focused on growth of an individual person’s learning. In a world where new technologies are always around the corner, more specialized personnel is needed and where we are reaching the limits to what we can do in many fields, future engineers face an uphill battle through their whole career. Staying in tune with the current standards is not an easy task, and one that requires more than just being told it or reading about it.

Lifelong learning, and actual learning at that, is a difficult process. Fortunately, rather than just being taught subjects, my professors over the years have taught me how to learn. The process is most evident in labs that I have taken as well as final projects. Being given a problem to solve that takes more than a few hours can be an overwhelming experience at first. One of the skills I have gained through my lab and project experience is to take things one step at a time and break things up in a productive and sequential way. This skill was most evident to me when I worked on a project during my internship and managed to complete it satisfactorily in the timeline I was told even though initially I felt the weight of the responsibility of being assigned a larger assignment like that.

Another large takeaway from my classes here is the way professors carried themselves when asked a question they did not initially know the answer to. There were multiple professors that, when asked a question during class that was outside the scope of the class, would pause and think before giving an answer or announce that they would look into it and come back to us. Seeing professors act that way helped me understand that it is acceptable to not know everything even if you might be expected to since it is unreasonable. Beyond that, you have a responsibility as an engineer to find answers when you are made aware you don’t know. This skill is one I applied as a Teaching Assistant for EE330. There were multiple times where I was asked questions that I did not know the answer to. My actions in these cases are a result of seeing my professors go through similar situations, since I would review class materials, past notes or find resources on my own to answer.

When a student goes through classes and receives high grades it is easy to fall in the trap of feeling like you know everything there is to know. In the last 4 years, I have learned that this is definitely not the case and there is always more to learn. This realization has fundamentally changed my learning process, since I try to make sure everything I learn is truly learned and not memorized. In addition to that, I try and add more breadth to my knowledge so that I am at least aware of different options. For me, this is most evident since I pursued a minor in Computer Science. As an Electrical Engineering student, I had to take a class on C programming and then a class later on where we applied that in an embedded systems course. I realized early on this is not enough exposure to the more programming side of things, so I decided to pursue the minor in order to spread out my experience and knowledge in a way that will make future learning and problem solving easier.

Overall, there are many fields where lifelong learning is necessary. In terms of Electrical Engineering, I believe after 4 years I have put myself in a situation where I can sustainably keep learning as new discoveries and standards come to be. Beyond that, through the experience I gained I am able to truly learn things rather than do so on a surface level.
